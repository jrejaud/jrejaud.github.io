After Arduino Part 3: Using an ATMega328P inside an electronics circuit

Vernacularly known as a "Standalone Arduino" or "Minimalist Arduino" or "Breadboard Arduino"

You should be familiar with the Fundamentals of Electronics before proceeding.

This page is a work in progress, follow me on twitter if you would like to know when I update it.

Overview

Want to learn how to build a circuit with a microcontroller in it? Do you want to take your ATMega328p out of Egypt off your Arduino Uno and have it still run the Arduino code you wrote? Alternatively, this guide also works with microcontrollers that have been programmed using AVR-C :)

Unlike a lot of "Breadboard Arduino" guides I've read, which just tells you to "plug stuff in here and do this", I will explain the theory and rational behind this circuit.

Removing the microcontroller (ATMega328P) from the breadboard

Key Prerequisites: Breadboards


Pop out the ATMega328P and put it on your breadboard. It is ornery like a Honey Badger and will not want to come out of its DIP Socket easily. But you must outsmart this chip and you must snatch victory by the balls.

Power Supply

Key Prerequisites: Electrical Power, Diodes, Capacitors, Integrated Circuits

When you connect your USB cable or a 2.1mm plug, you give power to your Arduino Board. But how much voltage and current do I need and how do I provide that power to the microcontroller?

According to our friend, the datasheet, an ATMega328P needs 1.8-5.5V (voltage) and ~0.2mA (current) in active mode.




The 5V Voltage Regulator will drop down a higher voltage (up to 35V) to 5V.

The 10uF capacitors let high frequency voltage changes go through to capacitor to ground and thus stops "ripples" in your power source from messing with your IC. Want more information on why this is important and how it works? High frequency noise suppression, alternatively AC seems to flow more easily through capacitors than DC.

The diode is important since the capacitors and IC are polar. Also, it makes sure that this doesn't happen.

Granted, these components aren't required, and for certain circuits you might not need them. For example, two AA batteries provides 3V, and that's enough to power an ATMega328P :)

Clock

Key Prerequisites: Fuses, Oscillators and Resonators, AVRDude (or some way to change the fuses)


An Arduino Uno Rev3 (Version 3, the latest one at the time of my writing this) uses a 16mHz external oscillator as its clock.

A singular ATMega328P gives you more freedom with your clock selection, with the help of a fuse calculator, you can choose:

  • Internal 1 mHz RC Oscillator
  • Internal 8 mHz RC Oscillator
  • Internal 16 kHz RC Oscillator
  • Internal 128 kHz RC Oscillator
  • External Clock

In this example, I am using a 16 mHz Resonator, since that's what the Arduino Uno uses. The outer pins of the resonator go to the crystal pins (9 and 10, direction doesn't matter) and the middle pin goes to GND.

Conclusion

That's the basics you need to get started! Program your chip in your Arduino Uno, pop it out and tada! Want to learn how to program your chip without an Uno, and other things? Go to the Learning Page
